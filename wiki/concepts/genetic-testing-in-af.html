Genetic Testing in Atrial Fibrillation

Genetic Testing in Atrial Fibrillation

Definition

Genetic testing in atrial fibrillation refers to the clinical or research-grade sequencing of cardiomyopathy and arrhythmia gene panels in patients with early-onset AF to identify disease-associated (pathogenic/likely pathogenic) variants that may signal an underlying inherited syndrome, guide additional diagnostic evaluation, and inform family screening.

Key Concepts

  • Current guideline status: As of 2021, major AF guidelines (AHA/ACC/HRS 2014; HRS/EHRA 2011) do not recommend routine genetic testing for AF. However, accumulating evidence has prompted a proposal to offer genetic testing to patients with early-onset AF after appropriate genetic counseling. (sources/eoaf-jama-2021)

  • Diagnostic yield: Approximately 10% of patients with AF diagnosed before age 66 carry a disease-associated variant on a 145-gene commercial arrhythmia/cardiomyopathy panel — rising to ~17% in those diagnosed before age 30. This is comparable to yields reported in other heritable cardiac conditions where testing is guideline-endorsed. (sources/eoaf-jama-2021)

  • Recommended age cutoff: No validated cutoff has been established. Prior proposals suggested age 45; this cohort study's data suggest genetic testing is reasonable up to age 60, with a stronger recommendation for those diagnosed before age 30 (where the yield is highest). (sources/eoaf-jama-2021)

  • Gene panel composition: Commercial panels typically include 145+ genes across cardiomyopathy (DCM, HCM, ARVC) and arrhythmia (LQTS, BrS, CPVT) syndromes. No AF-specific panel exists; current testing leverages overlap between AF and these inherited syndromes. (sources/eoaf-jama-2021)

  • ACMG variant classification: Variants are classified as benign, likely benign, VUS, likely pathogenic, or pathogenic. The VUS category is the most common result (~63% in EOAF), and managing VUS results requires expert genetic counseling to avoid undue patient anxiety or inappropriate clinical action. (sources/eoaf-jama-2021)

  • Clinical action when P/LP found: Detection of a disease-associated variant triggers syndrome-specific evaluation — for example, echocardiography for DCM/HCM, cardiac MRI for ARVC, Holter monitoring for LMNA-related conduction disease — as well as cascade family screening. (sources/eoaf-jama-2021)

  • Proposed pathway: Patients with early-onset AF referred to an inherited heart disease clinic → genetic counseling → commercial arrhythmia/cardiomyopathy panel testing → results management with cardiology and genetics. (sources/eoaf-jama-2021)

  • Prospective cohort yield for AF <40 without SHD (11.6% P/LP): In a prospective cohort of 122 patients with AF before age 40 without structural heart disease (mean age 31.2 years), 57% accepted genetic panel testing; 11.6% carried P/LP variants and 39.1% carried VUS. P/LP variants were predominantly cardiomyopathy genes: TTN ×4, LMNA ×2, PKP2 ×2, TNNI3 ×1. Among the 8 familial AF patients who underwent testing, all harbored at least one variant (3 P/LP, 5 VUS) — supporting a strong recommendation for genetic testing when family history is positive. (sources/eoaf-riskfactor-ehj-2026, rating: medium)

  • Very young AF without SHD still warrants genetic evaluation: The presence of cardiomyopathy gene P/LP variants in a cohort specifically designed to exclude SHD (LMNA, TTN, PKP2 in patients with normal CMR) confirms that structural normalcy at presentation does not exclude an underlying cardiomyopathy genotype. Genetic testing is particularly relevant before overt cardiomyopathy develops. (sources/eoaf-riskfactor-ehj-2026)

  • ESC 2021 HF guidelines — DCM minimum panel provides context for AF genetic testing: ESC 2021 mandates a minimum gene panel for all DCM/HNDC patients: TTN, LMNA, MYH7, MYPC, TNNT, RBM20, PLN, SCN5a, BAG3, and others. This panel substantially overlaps with the cardiomyopathy genes found in early-onset AF cohorts (TTN, LMNA, MYH7 identified in EOAF-JAMA 2021; TTN, LMNA, PKP2 in EOAF-riskfactor-EHJ-2026). The ESC 2021 panel operationally validates that cardiomyopathy gene testing is standard of care once DCM is suspected — EOAF patients who progress to DCM will have had some of these genes tested if tested at the AF stage. (sources/HF-ESC-2021, rating: very high)

  • AHA 2023 Class IIb recommendation for genetic testing in AF <45 years: AHA 2023 (Class IIb/B-NR) states genetic testing is reasonable in AF <45 years without obvious risk factors — this provides the only current formal guideline recommendation for genetic testing specifically in AF. ESC 2024 AF guidelines do not include a specific recommendation. ESC 2021 HF guidelines address genetic testing in the context of overt cardiomyopathy (DCM/HNDC), not isolated AF. The gap between these approaches remains a clinical challenge. (sources/HF-ESC-2021, sources/AF-AHA-2023)

  • UK Biobank population-level pLOF evidence (n = 403,990): WES-based gene-burden testing identified 6 genes (TTN, RPL3L, PKP2, CTNNA3, KDM5B, C10orf71; 5 replicated) with pLOF variants significantly associated with AF. This is the largest population-scale evidence base for the genetic architecture of AF, complementing clinic-based cohort studies. Carrier prevalence was 1.27% in the incident AF-free baseline population. (sources/Biobank-AF-JAMA-2024, rating: high)

  • Combined PRS + pLOF: OR 7.08 — multiplicative risk model: A 1-SD increase in the AF PRS carries OR 1.53; rare pLOF variant carrier status carries OR ~2–4 per gene; combined top-quintile PRS + pLOF variant → OR 7.08 (95% CI 6.03–8.28) for AF. This dose-response relationship across PRS quintiles × carrier strata provides the strongest evidence to date that both common and rare genetic variation should be integrated in AF risk stratification. (sources/Biobank-AF-JAMA-2024, rating: high)

  • 10-year absolute risk in high-risk combinations: Males >60 years + top PRS quintile + pLOF variant: 24% 10-year AF risk; equivalent females: 16%. Modifiable risk factors (BMI ≥30 + hypertension + top PRS) confer comparable absolute risk to pLOF carriers — illustrating that genetic and lifestyle risk are additive. (sources/Biobank-AF-JAMA-2024, rating: high)

  • AF as first manifestation of cardiomyopathy — pLOF evidence: In cause-specific Cox regression, pLOF carriers (predominantly TTNtv) had CM HR 3.13 before AF and CM HR 2.98 after AF — both largely TTN-driven. The AF PRS did not predict cardiomyopathy (HR 0.99). This population-level evidence confirms rare pLOF variant-positive AF is a cardiomyopathy-linked phenotype requiring cardiomyopathy surveillance, while high-PRS AF does not carry equivalent CM risk. (sources/Biobank-AF-JAMA-2024, rating: high)

Contradictions / Open Questions

  • No AF-specific gene panel — testing leverages cardiomyopathy/arrhythmia overlap: No dedicated AF gene panel exists. Current practice uses commercial panels designed for DCM, HCM, ARVC, LQTS, BrS, and CPVT — genes validated for ventricular disease are applied to an atrial phenotype. Variant classification and penetrance estimates from cardiomyopathy cohorts may not be directly transferable to AF-centric presentations. (sources/eoaf-jama-2021)
  • Guidelines not yet updated: As of 2021, major AF guidelines (AHA/ACC/HRS 2014; HRS/EHRA 2011) did not recommend routine genetic testing for AF. Clinical practice in this area is ahead of formal guideline adoption. Clinicians offering genetic testing to EOAF patients are acting on evidence that has not been incorporated into actionable Class I recommendations. (sources/eoaf-jama-2021)
  • VUS in familial AF: In 5 out of 8 familial AF patients who tested positive in the prospective <40 cohort, only a VUS was found — not a P/LP variant. The clinical value of segregation analyses in relatives for VUS reclassification in familial young AF is promising but not standardized. (sources/eoaf-riskfactor-ehj-2026)
  • UK Biobank pLOF associations: European ancestry only — limited generalizability: All UK Biobank WES data are from individuals of European ancestry. The population prevalence of pLOF variants in TTN, PKP2, CTNNA3, KDM5B, and RPL3L may differ substantially across ethnic groups. AF PRS weights derived from European GWAS are known to underperform in non-European populations. The OR 7.08 combined PRS + pLOF risk estimate cannot be extrapolated to non-European AF patients. (sources/Biobank-AF-JAMA-2024, rating: high)
  • TTN drives cardiomyopathy signal — non-TTN pLOF genes may not predict CM progression: When TTN variants are excluded from the UK Biobank analysis, the association of pLOF variants with cardiomyopathy and HF becomes non-significant. This raises the question of whether PKP2, CTNNA3, KDM5B, and RPL3L pLOF carriers face the same cardiomyopathy surveillance imperative as TTN carriers. Gene-specific surveillance strategies (CM monitoring for TTNtv; ARVC surveillance for PKP2) may be more appropriate than a uniform "any pLOF = CM surveillance" approach. (sources/Biobank-AF-JAMA-2024, rating: high)
